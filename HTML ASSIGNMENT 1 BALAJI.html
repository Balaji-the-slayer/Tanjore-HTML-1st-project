About Tanjavur


History

This place was previously called Siyali. Indra once fled to this place in fear of the asura called Surapadma and did tapas here There are no references to Thanjavur in the Sangam period (third century BCE to fourth century CE) Tamil records, though some scholars believe that the city has existed since that time. Kovil Venni, situated 15 miles (24 km) to the east of the city, was the site of the Battle of Venni between the Chola king Karikala and a confederacy of the Cheras and the Pandyas. The Cholas seemed to have faced an invasion of the Kalabhras in the third century CE after which the kingdom faded into obscurity. The region around present day Thanjavur was conquered by the Mutharayars during the sixth century, who ruled it up to 849. The Cholas came to prominence once more through the rise of the Medieval Chola monarch Vijayalaya (841–878 CE) in about 850 CE. Vijayalaya conquered Thanjavur from the Mutharayar king Elango Mutharayar and built a temple dedicated to the Hindu goddess Nisumbhasudani. His son Aditya I (871–901) consolidated their hold over the city. The Rashtrakuta king Krishna II (878–914), a contemporary of the Chola king Parantaka I (907–950), claims to have conquered Thanjavur, but there are no records to support the claim. Gradually, Thanjavur became the most important city in the Chola Empire and remained its capital till the emergence of Gangaikonda Cholapuram in about 1025. During the first decade of the eleventh century, the Chola king Raja Raja Chola I (985–1014) constructed the Brihadeeswarar Temple at Thanjavur. The temple is considered to be one of the best specimens of Tamil architecture Thanjavur was successfully conquered in 1674 by Ekoji I (1675–84), the Maratha feudatory of the sultan of Bijapur and half-brother of Shivaji (1627/30-80) of the Bhonsle dynasty. Ekoji founded the Thanjavur Maratha kingdom which ruled Thanjavur till 1855. The Marathas exercised their sovereignty over Thanjavur throughout the last quarter of the 17th and the whole of the 18th century. The Maratha rulers patronized Carnatic music. In 1787, Amar Singh, the regent of Thanjavur, deposed the minor Raja, his nephew Serfoji II (1787–93) and captured the throne. Serfoji II was restored in 1799 with the assistance of the British, who induced him to relinquish the administration of the kingdom and left him in charge of Thanjavur fort and surrounding areas. The kingdom was eventually absorbed into British India in 1855 by the Doctrine of Lapse when Shivaji II (1832–55), the last Thanjavur Maratha ruler, died without a legitimate male heir. The British referred to the city as Tanjore in their records. Five years after its annexation, the British replaced Negapatam (modern-day Nagapattinam) with Thanjavur as the seat of the district administration. Under the British, Thanjavur emerged as an important regional centre. The 1871 India census recorded a population of 52,171, making Thanjavur the third largest city in the Madras Presidency. After India's independence, Thanjavur continued as the district headquarters.

Culture

Tanjavur’s cultural heritage is further enriched by its exquisite Tanjore paintings, characterized by intricate detailing and vibrant colors, as well as its distinctive silk weaving industry, which produces some of the finest silk sarees in India. The cuisine of Tanjavur is a delightful array of South Indian flavors, with traditional dishes like dosa, idli, and sambar forming the heart of its culinary landscape. Festivals, such as the annual Thanjavur Festival, bring the city’s cultural vibrancy to life, celebrating its rich traditions with music, dance, and communal feasts. Overall, Tanjavur is a living canvas of South Indian heritage, where history and culture blend seamlessly to offer a unique and immersive experience



Art & Handicrafts

Tanjore Paintings: One of the most famous crafts from Tanjavur, these paintings are known for their intricate detailing, vibrant colors, and use of gold foil. They often depict Hindu deities, mythological themes, and religious icons. The technique involves layering a base of plaster of Paris on a wooden frame, followed by meticulous detailing with natural colors and embellishments.

Thanjavur painting is a classical South Indian painting style, originating in the town of Thanjavur (anglicized as Tanjore) in Tamil Nadu. The art form draws its immediate resources and inspiration from way back about 1600 AD, a period when the Nayakas of Thanjavur under the suzerainty of the Vijayanagara Rayas encouraged art—chiefly, classical dance and music—as well as literature, both in Telugu and Tamil and painting of chiefly Hindu religious subjects in temples. However, it can safely be surmised that Thanjavur painting, as we know it now, originated in the Maratha court of Thanjavur (1676–1855). It has been recognized as a Geographical indication by the Government of India in 2007–08. Thanjavur paintings are characterised by rich and vivid colours, simple iconic composition, glittering gold foils overlaid on delicate but extensive gesso work and inlay of glass beads and pieces or very rarely precious and semi-precious gems. In Thanjavur paintings one can see the influence of Deccani, Vijayanagara, Maratha and even European or Company styles of painting. Essentially serving as devotional icons, the subjects of most paintings are Hindu gods, goddesses, and saints. Episodes from Hindu Puranas, Sthala-puranas and other religious texts were visualised, sketched or traced and painted with the main figure or figures placed in the central section of the picture (mostly within an architecturally delineated space such as a mantapa or prabhavali) surrounded by several subsidiary figures, themes and subjects. There are also many instances when Jain, Sikh, Muslim, other religious and even secular subjects were depicted in Tanjore paintings. Sikh Gurus with Bhai Bala and Bhai Mardana – 20th century Thanjavur paintings are panel paintings done on wooden planks, and hence referred to as palagai padam (palagai = "wooden plank"; padam = "picture") in local parlance. In modern times, these paintings have become souvenirs for festive occasions in South India.

Carnatic music & Classical Dance forms

The Tanjavur style of Bharatanatyam, originating from Tanjavur in Tamil Nadu, is a revered and classical dance form that holds a significant place in Indian cultural heritage. This style is distinguished by its elaborate and expressive performance techniques, which have been refined over centuries. It emphasizes intricate hand gestures (mudras), expressive facial expressions (abhinaya), and precise footwork, all of which work together to narrate mythological stories and convey deep emotions. In the Tanjavur style, the dance repertoire is traditionally divided into specific segments: the Alarippu, which serves as a rhythmic warm-up; the Varnam, a complex and dramatic piece showcasing the dancer’s skill and emotional range; and the Tillana, a lively and rhythmic conclusion. Each component is performed with a focus on maintaining classical aesthetics and intricate detailing. The Tanjavur style is also known for its vibrant costumes, typically featuring richly embroidered silk sarees, and elaborate jewelry, enhancing the visual appeal of the performance. The dance form’s historical roots trace back to the royal patronage of the Chola and Nayak dynasties, which played a crucial role in its development and preservation. Today, it continues to be a celebrated form of classical dance, both in India and internationally, reflecting its enduring artistic and cultural significance.

Tanjavur, a historical city in Tamil Nadu, is a vibrant hub of Carnatic music, a classical music tradition that has flourished in South India for centuries. Known for its rich musical heritage, Tanjavur has been instrumental in shaping the evolution of Carnatic music, which is characterized by its intricate rhythms, melodic structures, and devotional themes.

Carnatic Music Culture:

The city’s deep-rooted connection to Carnatic music is largely attributed to the patronage of the Chola and Nayak dynasties, which significantly influenced the development and popularization of this art form. Tanjavur is home to the legendary composers of the Trinity of Carnatic Music—Thyagaraja, Muthuswami Dikshitar, and Shyama Sastri—whose compositions remain central to the Carnatic repertoire. The city’s traditional music houses and institutions continue to preserve and teach these classical compositions, maintaining the rich musical lineage.


Events and Festivals:

Tanjavur hosts several important Carnatic music events and festivals throughout the year. One of the most notable is the **Tanjavur Music Festival**, which attracts prominent musicians and enthusiasts from across India and the world. This festival celebrates Carnatic music through a series of concerts, lecture-demonstrations, and workshops, providing a platform for both established artists and emerging talents. Additionally, the **Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan**, a cultural institution in Tanjavur, regularly organizes music concerts and cultural programs that highlight the region’s musical traditions. The **Thanjavur Margazhi Festival** also showcases Carnatic music performances, particularly during the month of Margazhi (December-January), a period considered auspicious for classical music and dance. Overall, Tanjavur's Carnatic music culture is a testament to the city’s historical significance and ongoing contribution to classical music. Its festivals and events play a crucial role in preserving and promoting this cherished tradition, continuing to inspire and engage audiences worldwide.

Brihadeshwara Temple Peruvudaiyar Kovil




An Architectural Triumph of the Chola Dynasty


Construction and Commission:
The Brihadisvara Temple was commissioned by Rajaraja Chola I, who ruled the Chola Empire from 985 to 1014 CE. It was constructed between 1003 and 1010 CE. The temple was built to serve as the primary center of worship for the deity Shiva, and it symbolized the Chola dynasty's power and prosperity.


Architectural Features:
The Brihadisvara Temple at Thanjavur, also known as Peruvudaiyar Kovil, is a remarkable testament to Chola architectural and engineering prowess. The Brihadisvara Temple at Thanjavur, also known as Peruvudaiyar Kovil, is a remarkable testament to Chola architectural and engineering prowess. Built between 1003 and 1010 CE by Rajaraja Chola I, it features several extraordinary elements. The temple’s central dome, or Vimana, stands 66 meters (216 feet) high and is topped by an 80-ton granite capstone, transported from a distant quarry using ingenious techniques. The 13-tiered gopuram (gateway tower) adds to the temple's grandeur and serves as a dramatic entrance. Inside, the sanctum houses a massive Shiva Lingam, 3.6 meters (12 feet) tall, symbolizing Shiva. The temple’s mandapa (pillared hall) is supported by intricately carved pillars adorned with detailed sculptures of Hindu deities and mythological scenes, showcasing exceptional craftsmanship. Constructed entirely from granite, the temple demonstrates advanced engineering techniques and precision in stone cutting. An architectural marvel of the Brihadisvara Temple is the optical illusion of the Vimana’s height, designed to appear perfectly aligned from all angles. Additionally, some believe the temple's design incorporates astronomical and mathematical principles, reflecting the advanced knowledge of the Chola architects. The Brihadisvara Temple remains a symbol of Chola architectural brilliance and cultural heritage.


Cultural and Religious Significance:
The Brihadisvara Temple was a central religious and cultural hub in the Chola Empire. It was not only a place of worship but also a symbol of Rajaraja Chola I’s devotion to Shiva and his commitment to the Chola state's grandeur. The temple's design incorporates advanced engineering techniques and reflects the zenith of Chola architectural achievement.


Legacy:
The temple remains a testament to Chola architectural brilliance and has been designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the "Great Living Chola Temples." It continues to attract scholars, historians, and tourists for its historical, architectural, and artistic significance.




Brihadisvara Temple Gangaikonda Cholapuram


A Monument of Chola Grandeur

The Brihadisvara Temple at Gangaikonda Cholapuram, crafted between 1015 and 1035 CE by Rajendra Chola I, is a masterpiece of Chola architecture. Constructed to rival the Thanjavur temple, it embodies the zenith of Chola grandeur and artistic prowess. The temple’s central dome (Vimana), rising 55 meters (180 feet), is adorned with a colossal granite capstone, showcasing the remarkable engineering feats of the period. The majestic 9-tiered gopuram (gateway tower) adds to the temple's splendor, providing a regal entrance.


Inside, the sanctum enshrines a monumental Shiva Lingam, embodying the divine. The pillared hall (mandapa) features exquisitely carved granite pillars, rich with intricate depictions of Hindu deities and mythological tales. The temple’s design, with its precise alignment and optical illusion of balance, reflects advanced astronomical knowledge and architectural sophistication. The Brihadisvara Temple at Gangaikonda Cholapuram stands as a timeless symbol of Chola artistic and cultural brilliance.



Thanjavur Palace (Aranmanai)





Historical Background

The Thanjavur Palace, or Aranmanai, was initially built by the Nayak rulers in the 16th century and expanded by the Marathas in the late 17th century. It served as the residence and administrative center for the Maratha kings, including members of the Shivaji family. The palace reflects the political and cultural shifts in South India during this period.


Architectural Features

The palace showcases Indo-Saracenic architecture with Dravidian and Mughal influences. Key features include expansive courtyards, a grand Durbar Hall with ornate wooden rafters, and a distinctive clock tower added during the British colonial era. The use of sandstone and granite, along with detailed carvings, highlights the artistic achievements of the era.


Present-Day Significance

Now a major tourist attraction, the Thanjavur Palace houses the Rajaraja Chola Art Gallery, displaying bronze sculptures and artifacts. The museum offers insights into the region's history and heritage, making the palace a key site for cultural preservation and education. It continues to attract visitors with its historical charm and architectural beauty.





Tanjore Art Gallery



The Tanjore Art Gallery, housed within the historic Thanjavur Palace, is a treasure trove of South Indian art and cultural artifacts. Among its many exhibits, the Tanjavur Thalayatti Bommai, or "Head-Wobbling Doll," stands out as a remarkable example of traditional craftsmanship from the Tanjore region. The Thalayatti Bommai is a unique handcrafted doll that has captivated visitors with its intricate artistry and innovative design. The doll is renowned for its head-wobbling mechanism, which is achieved through a sophisticated balance of weight and movement. This clever engineering allows the doll's head to sway gently when the doll is touched, creating a charming and whimsical effect. The head-wobbling feature exemplifies the ingenuity of South Indian artisans in combining function with art.

Crafted from a combination of wood, clay, and metal, the Thalayatti Bommai is meticulously adorned with traditional South Indian attire, including richly embroidered sarees and intricate jewelry. The vibrant colors and detailed decorations reflect the cultural richness of the Tanjore region and the artistic sensibilities of its craftsmen. Each doll is a testament to the high level of skill involved in its creation, highlighting the importance of craftsmanship in South Indian art traditions. The Tanjore Art Gallery’s display of the Thalayatti Bommai not only serves as a showcase of this unique artifact but also emphasizes the broader context of Tanjore's artistic heritage. The gallery itself is dedicated to preserving and promoting South Indian art forms, including bronze sculptures, paintings, and traditional crafts. The Thalayatti Bommai remains a symbol of the region’s rich cultural legacy and continues to attract interest from visitors eager to explore the depth and diversity of South Indian artistry.




Getting There: A Comprehensive Guide to Reaching Tanjavur


How to get there ?

By Air:

The nearest airport to Tanjavur is Tiruchirapalli International Airport (TRZ), located about 60 kilometers away. This airport serves both domestic and international flights, with connections to major Indian cities like Chennai, Bengaluru, and Mumbai, as well as some international destinations. From the airport, you can hire a taxi or take a pre-booked car service to Tanjavur. The drive typically takes around 1.5 to 2 hours. Alternatively, you can opt for a bus service that runs between the airport and Tanjavur.

By Train:

Tanjavur is well-connected by the Indian Railways network. The Tanjavur Railway Station is a major junction with services linking it to several key cities across South India. You can book train tickets from cities like Chennai, Bengaluru, and Mumbai directly to Tanjavur. Trains such as the Cholan Express, Kumbakonam Express, and the Rameswaram Express frequently service this route. Booking in advance is recommended, especially during peak travel seasons.

By Bus:

Several state-run and private bus services operate to Tanjavur from neighboring cities and towns. The Tamil Nadu State Transport Corporation (TNSTC) provides regular services to Tanjavur from Chennai, Coimbatore, and other major cities. Private operators also offer luxury and semi-luxury bus options, providing a comfortable journey with amenities such as air-conditioning and refreshments. The main bus station in Tanjavur is centrally located, making it easy to access various parts of the city.

Travel Tips:

Book in Advance: For trains and flights, booking in advance ensures better availability and fare options.

Local Transport: Auto-rickshaws, taxis, and rental cars are readily available for local travel once you reach Tanjavur.

Plan Your Route: Familiarize yourself with the route and travel times to ensure a smooth journey.
